Through systematic experiments across representative UMM architectures, we find a clear architectural distinction between models that exhibit bi-directional transferability and those that do not. Multimodal understanding and image generation have each made remarkable progress, but largely in isolation. This naturally motivates the construction of Unified Multimodal Models (UMMs) that integrate both capabilities within a single architecture. Recent UMMs achieve performance comparable to dedicated expert models in both tasks, suggesting that unification incurs little compromise. Yet a fundamental question remains: "do these models truly unify the two capabilities, or do they merely host two independent systems within shared parameters?'' If understanding and generation are genuinely integrated, knowledge acquired through one task should be accessible to the other. However, whether such cross-task knowledge sharing actually occurs in current UMMs has not been directly examined.

Several prior works have explored interactions between understanding and generation in multimodal models. However, these studies typically measure synergy through aggregate performance changes on general benchmarks, which do not directly reveal whether knowledge is actually shared across tasks. A performance gain from joint training could stem from regularization effects or increased data exposure rather than genuine knowledge transfer. To address this, we propose a more direct measurement: we train a model on a specific task in one modality alone and test whether the other modality improves on the same task without any explicit supervision. We term this property \textit{transferability}, and adopt it as a concrete indicator of cross-task knowledge integration in UMMs. 

We begin by investigating whether transferability exists in current open-weight UMMs. To this end, we categorize existing architectures into four representative paradigms and select a model from each. Using counting as a controlled visual concept, we train understanding with counting visual question–answer data and generation with counting-aware text–image pairs, and then measure how well numerical concepts transfer to the untrained modality. Through these experiments, we identify architectures where transferability is inherent and others where it is absent. In particular, models in which understanding and generation share a unified visual encoder within a shared transformer exhibit strong bi-directional transferability, whereas architectures lacking this strong structural coupling show limited transferability.

We then turn to a natural follow-up question: when transferability emerges, is it consistently bi-directional across tasks? Prior works suggests that understanding objectives tend to promote high-level semantic representations, while generation objectives often capture fine-grained visual structures. This distinction raises the possibility that transferability may not behave uniformly across tasks. To examine this, we select representative tasks with distinct visual characteristics and evaluate transfer in both directions. Our experiments show that transfer is neither uniform nor symmetric across tasks. We find that tasks relying on coarse, image-level semantics exhibit strong bi-directional transferability, whereas tasks requiring fine-grained visual discrimination show more limited and asymmetric transfer.
