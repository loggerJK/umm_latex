Multimodal understanding and image generation have each made remarkable progress in recent years, yet they have mostly evolved in isolation. This separation has recently motivated the development of Unified Multimodal Models (UMMs), which aim to integrate both capabilities within a single architecture. Recent UMMs demonstrate that such unification can achieve performance comparable to specialized experts for each task~\cite{deng2025bagel, }.
However, despite this progress, it remains unclear how the two capabilities interact once they are trained within a single model. Specifically, the potential benefits that arise from jointly modeling understanding and generation remain largely unexplored.

Several recent studies have begun to investigate interactions between understanding and generation in multimodal systems. For example, some works demonstrate that semantic representations from understanding can improve the semantic fidelity of generation, while fine-grained visual representations learned through generation may enhance grounding and compositional reasoning \cite{wu2026liquid,xin2025lumina,yang2025mmar,wu2025harmonizing,kou2025orthus,li2025dual,zhang2025unified}.  Despite these insights, existing analyses typically examine interactions between understanding and generation through aggregate performance changes on general benchmarks. While these results provide useful high-level observations, they do not directly reveal how knowledge is shared between the two capabilities. Furthermore, because such evaluations rely on broad benchmark improvements, it remains difficult to determine whether the observed gains stem from genuine cross-task knowledge transfer or from indirect factors such as additional supervision or regularization effects. 

We address this gap by studying a more direct and actionable signal of cross-task interaction: \emph{transferability}.
Instead of inferring interaction from aggregate benchmark gains, we examine whether training a given task (\textit{e.g.,} counting) in one modality (understanding or generation) reliably improves the other modality on the same task, under controlled interventions. Through a series of experiments, we find that such cross-modal transfer emerges in current UMMs, and this study leads to two key findings: (i) the existence and strength of transferability vary substantially across models, revealing a strong dependence on architectural design; and  (ii) when transfer is present, it can be exploited as a practical mechanism to improve targeted generative capabilities by training on the corresponding understanding task.

Specifically, we begin with a controlled analysis using \textit{counting} as a probe task. Across several representative Unified Multimodal Models~\cite{xin2025lumina,chen2025januspro,chen2505blip3,deng2025bagel}, we observe that transferability strongly depends on architectural design. In particular, models that employ a fully shared transformer backbone along with a unified visual tokenizer exhibit consistent transfer between modalities, whereas architectures that leverage a separated backbone or a tokenizer show little or no transfer. These results suggest that the degree of representation sharing plays a critical role in enabling cross-task knowledge transfer. Moreover, in architectures where such transfer emerges, we find that training on one modality alone can yield meaningful performance gains in the other, even without any explicit supervision for the target modality.

Building on this observation, we investigate whether transferability can be used as a practical mechanism for improving generative capabilities using a UMM with a shared backbone and tokenizer, \textit{i.e.} Lumina-Dimoo~\cite{xin2025lumina}. Directly training generation to acquire a specific capability such as accurately counting objects can be challenging, as generation must simultaneously preserve image quality and the underlying data distribution. Instead, we find that training the corresponding \textbf{understanding task} can effectively introduce the capability into generation through transfer. Extensive experiments across multiple visual capabilities, including counting, spatial relation reasoning, and OCR, confirm that cross-modal transfer can consistently improve generation performance on these tasks while preserving

At the same time, we observe that the degree of transfer varies across tasks, highlighting differences in how visual capabilities are represented across modalities.
This strategy allows generation performance to improve on the target capability while largely preserving generative quality. 

Our contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We introduce \textbf{transferability} as a tool for analyzing cross-task interactions in Unified Multimodal Models, and show that capabilities learned in understanding or generation can transfer across modalities.
    \item Through controlled experiments, we demonstrate that transferability strongly depends on architectural design, emerging most clearly in models with fully shared transformer backbones.
    \item We show that transferability can be leveraged as a practical strategy for improving targeted generative capabilities without degrading generative quality, and validate this approach across counting, spatial reasoning, and OCR tasks.
\end{itemize}
