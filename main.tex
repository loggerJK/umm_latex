\begin{abstract}
Unified Multimodal Models (UMMs) integrate understanding and generation within a single architecture, yet how the two tasks interact remains understudied. We investigate \textbf{transferability} in UMMs: whether training a capability on one task improves the same capability on the other without explicit supervision. Through controlled experiments, we find that transferability strongly depends on architecture—models with fully shared backbones and a unified visual tokenizer exhibit consistent cross-task transfer, while loosely coupled designs show little or none. Building on this, we demonstrate a practical strategy: instead of directly fine-tuning generation for a target capability, training the corresponding understanding task and letting the knowledge transfer into generation improves task-specific performance while preserving overall generative quality. We validate this across counting, spatial reasoning, and OCR, showing that cross-task transfer can be systematically exploited in UMMs.

% Unified Multimodal Models (UMMs)  aim to integrate multimodal understanding and image generation within a single architecture. While recent models demonstrate strong performance on both tasks, it remains understudied how the two objectives interact once they are jointly trained. In this work, we study \textbf{transferability} as a direct indicator of cross-task interaction in UMMs. Specifically, we investigate whether training a capability in one modality (understanding or generation) can improve performance on the same capability in the other modality without explicit supervision through controlled experiments. We then find that transferability strongly depends on architectural design: models with fully shared transformer backbones and unified visual tokenizer exhibit consistent cross-modal transfer, while loosely coupled architectures show little or no transfer. Building on this observation, we demonstrate that transferability can be exploited as a practical mechanism for improving generative capabilities. Instead of directly training generation for a target capability, we train the corresponding understanding task and allow the knowledge to transfer into generation. This approach improves task-specific generation performance while largely preserving generative quality, which is often difficult to achieve through direct generation fine-tuning. We validate this phenomenon across multiple visual capabilities, including counting, spatial relation reasoning, and OCR. Our results show that cross-modal transfer is not merely an artifact of joint training, but a property that can be systematically analyzed and leveraged in Unified Multimodal Models.
